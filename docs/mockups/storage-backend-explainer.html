How ResearchOS storage works

explainer draft for lab onboarding + wiki

Your notebook is yours. The cloud is an optional, shared lab allowance.

ResearchOS is local-first at every tier: your notebook runs on your own machine and your data lives in a folder you own. The cloud is only an optional layer for sync, sharing, and collaboration. A whole lab shares one allowance, and only the lab head ever pays.

1Your data lives on your computer

Notes, experiments, methods, sequences, images, files, all of it is plain files in a folder you own. ResearchOS reads and writes that folder, and that folder is always the original. Private notes never upload. Only what you sync, share, or co-edit ever gets a cloud copy.

The cloud OPTIONAL · ENCRYPTED

A thin layer for sync, sharing, and collaboration.
  • Holds copies only of what you sync or share
  • Encrypted in transit and at rest; a one-time Send is also end-to-end
  • Your private, working data still lives on your disk
  • Turn it off and the notebook still works

Takeaway for the lab: the size limits below are NOT a limit on your research. They only apply to the optional cloud copies used for sync, sharing, and collaboration.

2Three ways data can leave your machine

Each path handles encryption differently, so it is worth being precise. Only the third, real-time collaboration, is what counts against your storage allowance.

Private, on your diskYour own notes and files never leave your machine. Nothing uploads unless you choose one of the two paths below.
A one-time SendSend a note, method, project, or a phone photo to someone. End-to-end encrypted and transient, deleted the moment they pick it up. Does not count against the storage pool.
Real-time collaborationSo collaborators converge even when offline, the server holds the shared copy. Encrypted in transit and at rest, but not end-to-end. This is the copy that counts against the allowance.

3Your lab's shared storage allowance

The cloud copies count against a free allowance. The key fact: a lab shares one allowance, no matter how many members join, and only the lab head pays. It is not per-person.

4What happens if a lab hits the cap?

This is the part to reassure the lab about. Reaching the cap simply pauses cloud sync until space is freed or the lab head adds storage. It never surprise-bills, and it never loses data: your edits keep saving to your own disk the whole time.

Plenty of room in the shared pool.
Cap reached means paused, not charged. Cloud sync stops; nobody is billed by surprise. There is no monthly meter, your footprint simply sits at the ceiling until it comes down. To resume, free up space or the PI adds storage. Your data is never at risk, it lives on your machine.

Separate from the pool, a one-time Send to you lands in a small per-user inbox (not the shared allowance) and clears itself the moment you import it, or after 30 days, so transient transfers never pile up.

A bench or gel photo is about the size of a still from a modern phone, roughly 3 to 5 MB depending on the device and scene (call it ~4 MB). A shared project varies a lot with its attachments, a few MB for a text-only project, hundreds of MB if it is photo-heavy. Files are whatever you attach, a few KB to about a gigabyte.

7How it is paid for

The local-first core is free and open source (AGPLv3) for every lab, with every feature included. It is supported by a UW-Madison RISE fellowship and voluntary contributions. Cloud storage above the free pool comes as flat-price plans, priced to recover what it costs us to run, not a profit center.

If a lab leans on the cloud heavily, the lab head can move the lab to a larger flat-price plan. Solo users stay free, supported by the fellowship and by labs that choose to give back.

Why it stays cheap: our provider storage runs from about $0.015 per gigabyte-month for the bulk pool up to about $0.35 for the small database tier, so a 5 GB free pool costs us pennies and the paid plans only recover real cost. Plan prices are still being finalized from real usage, so they are not published yet.
