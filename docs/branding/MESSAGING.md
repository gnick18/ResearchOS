## Audience

- **Graduate students and postdocs.** They do the daily bench work and live in the notebook. They are paid little, move between labs, and fear losing their data when they leave. They convert on free, on ownership, and on "I can take it with me."
- **Principal investigators and lab heads.** They are accountable for the lab's data, funding, and compliance, and they hold the budget. They are skeptical of recurring per-seat costs and of trusting a startup's cloud with a decade of work. They convert on cost, on the compliance answer, and on members adopting it willingly.
- **Lab managers and core-facility staff.** They run shared protocols, inventory, and onboarding. They want one source of truth that survives turnover. They convert on durability and on one tool replacing many.
- **Department and institution administrators.** They buy the site licenses today. They want lower cost and real adoption, plus a credible security and ownership story. They convert on a trust packet, standard terms, and a price that does not scale painfully with headcount.

## Long Description (3 versions)

**Version A (ownership-led).**
ResearchOS is a free, open-source electronic lab notebook for researchers who do not want to hand their work to a vendor. Every note, method, experiment, structure, and file is a plain file in a folder you own on your own computer. Nothing uploads unless you choose to sync, share, or collaborate, so private work never leaves your machine and the app keeps working offline. It replaces a stack of expensive single-purpose tools, the notebook, the chemistry drawer, the cloning tool, and the stats package, with one app that is free for your whole lab. The science is open and validated in public, so you can trust the numbers you cite. The only thing that ever costs money is the optional cloud storage you choose, priced at what it costs us to run.

**Version B (cost-led).**
Lab software bills you hundreds of dollars per person per year, and your lab pays it again for every student who rotates through. ResearchOS is free and open source instead. The whole lab gets a real electronic lab notebook, a chemistry drawer, a cloning tool, and a validated stats package in one app, with nothing per seat. Your data is not the price. It lives in a folder you own on your own disk, so leaving is just closing a folder, not a migration fight. If you want sync, sharing, or collaboration, optional cloud storage is the only paid part, and it is priced at cost for individuals and labs.

**Version C (trust-led).**
Closed research tools ask you to trust a cloud you cannot see and a stats engine you cannot check. ResearchOS does the opposite. It is open source, so anyone can read exactly how their data is stored and computed, and a public transparency page shows our results matching Biopython, primer3, R, and Prism. Your research stays in plain files on your own computer, which is also the honest answer to an NIH data-management plan. It is free for every researcher, built and shaped by a UW Distinguished Research Fellowship at UW-Madison, and run as a real, accountable business rather than a hobby link.

## Outcome Pillars

1. **Ownership**
2. **One Workspace**
3. **Trust**

---

### Pillar 1: Ownership

**Pain points**
- My data is trapped in a vendor's cloud and leaving means a migration fight.
- I pay every year just to keep accessing work I already did.
- My NIH data plan has no honest answer for where the data lives and who controls it.

**Benefits**
- Your research lives in a folder you own, readable with or without ResearchOS.
- Leaving is just closing a folder, because export is the default state.
- You can tell a funder exactly where the data lives, because it is on your machine.

**Details**
- A local folder of plain files via the File System Access API.
- One-click portable export of a single user's work.
- Local-first by architecture, so nothing uploads unless you choose to.

**Proof points**
- Open source on GitHub, so the storage format is readable by anyone.
- The folder opens and is usable outside ResearchOS.
- Works fully offline, with no server dependency.

---

### Pillar 2: One Workspace

**Pain points**
- I pay for a separate tool for the notebook, chemistry, cloning, and stats.
- The licenses stack up and renew every year for the whole lab.
- Switching between tools loses context and history.

**Benefits**
- One free app spans the notebook, chemistry, sequences, and stats.
- The whole lab is covered with no per-seat bill.
- Everything links together in one place, so context is not lost.

**Details**
- Chemistry Workbench (Ketcher plus RDKit, PubChem import, literature search).
- A SnapGene-style sequence editor for cloning workflows.
- The Data Hub statistics engine with custom lab calculators and method templates.

**Proof points**
- Replaces tools that cost from a few hundred to over a thousand dollars each.
- All features are free, with no paid tier on the app itself.
- One shared lab pool where only the PI pays and members never see a bill.

---

### Pillar 3: Trust

**Pain points**
- I cannot verify that a closed stats or Tm tool computes the right number.
- I am asked to trust a startup's cloud with a decade of irreplaceable work.
- I worry a free tool will get bought, raise prices, or disappear.

**Benefits**
- You can read exactly how your data is stored and computed.
- You can cite results you are able to verify yourself.
- You can rely on a real, accountable business rather than a hobby link.

**Details**
- The public /transparency page recomputes Tm, alignment, and digest results against Biopython and primer3, as a test gate.
- AGPLv3 open source, so trust is verifiable rather than promised.
- A cost circuit breaker pauses cloud writes before a runaway bill, and the local app keeps working.

**Proof points**
- Grew out of work begun during a UW-Madison Distinguished Research Fellowship.
- A real merchant of record with banking and Stripe set up.
- Solidarity pricing, where larger institutions subsidize free use for individual researchers.
