What we built
It started as a tool for one lab and grew, one feature at a time, into a full platform. Here is how that happened.
Where it came from

How it started

ResearchOS began as a tool for one person. I wanted my own lab notes in plain files on my own computer, not locked inside someone else's cloud.

Then a labmate wanted to use it. So I built the next thing we needed, and then the next. Every feature came from a real problem in front of me, never from a roadmap.

There was never a plan to build a platform. It grew one feature at a time, and four months later it had quietly replaced five separate tools. That part was an accident.

PART TWO
Where your data lives
Local by default, cloud only if you want it, and priced at our actual cost.
Local-first

Your data is just files on your computer

A folder you can open in FinderNotes are Markdown, records are JSON. Readable with or without ResearchOS.
No account, no upload, works offlineThe full notebook runs with nothing in the cloud. Your everyday work never leaves your machine.
Sync it your own wayDrop the folder in Drive, Dropbox, iCloud, or Box and your existing backup handles it.
This is why the core is freeBecause we never have to store or process your work, we never have to charge you to use it.
ResearchOS runs in Chrome or Edge on the desktop, the browsers that let an app work directly with a folder on your disk.
Optional cloud

Cloud storage is optional, and priced at cost

Off by defaultTurn it on only if you want cross-device sync, sharing, or an off-site backup. Nothing moves to the cloud until you say so.
5 GB free, then a low flat planEvery account gets a 5 GB cloud pool at no cost, enough for most labs. Above it, storage is a low flat monthly plan priced to recover our cost, not a per-gigabyte meter that creeps up.
Editing is never metered per keystrokeHeavy real-time use simply slows to periodic sync. It never turns into a surprise bill.
A hard cost ceilingA built-in circuit breaker caps spend. If it is reached, cloud writes pause and the local app keeps working without interruption.
Free during the betaCloud billing is not turned on yet, so the whole app is free today while we finish.
Cloud architecture

How the cloud layers work

Your computer
The original. Every note and file, as plain files you own.
$0 · offline · private
files & images
Cloudflare R2
bulk file bytes
$0.015per GB-mo, free egress
Images and attachments land here, never in a database. The bulk of your bytes, kept on the cheapest layer.
tiny edits
Durable Objects
live co-editing
$0.20per GB-mo + activity
Only tiny CRDT deltas and the live sync socket. Powers real-time co-editing in a lab, kept small on purpose.
a sealed send
Encrypted relay
one-time send
1 GB30 days, then gone
A sealed handoff to another user, deleted the moment they pick it up. End-to-end encrypted, and it never counts against your pool.
R2-first. We push the heavy bytes to the cheapest layer and keep only tiny, hot state in the pricey one, so a full 5 GB free pool costs us under a dime a month. The cloud only ever holds copies. Your folder stays the original, and a collaborator's work mirrors back to your own disk.
Real-time collaboration and billing are flag-gated and off during the free beta.

Accounts

Why make an account? It is optional

You never need one to use the notebook. An account does not move your data to the cloud, it adds identity and connection on top of the local-first core.

Prove it is yours

Your work is attributed to your ORCID identity, so authorship is clear and verifiable.

Never lose access

Recovery codes mean a lost or broken laptop does not mean lost access to your account.

Work anywhere

Pick up your work on another computer, or capture from your phone at the bench.

Send and receive

Share a note, method, or sequence with any other user by email, even with no shared folder.

Join a lab

Automatic sharing with your lab, shared methods, and PI oversight when you want it.

Still local-first

Your folder stays the source of truth. The account is connection, not a cloud handover.

The business behind it

Why there's an LLC

Optional cloud storage costs real money every month, so there is a small, accountable entity to collect it, pay the bills, and handle taxes. Priced to sustain the free tiers, not to profit.

Behind the scenes

The dashboards that run it

Two operator-only pages let one person run the business. Aggregate numbers only, never anyone's data, and they refuse to bill a real customer until the state tax question is answered.

Cost circuit breaker

Caps the monthly cloud bill. If variable cost hits the budget, cloud writes pause and the local app keeps working. Reset is manual.

Infrastructure capacity

Free-tier headroom for each service, with a survival-risk banner that turns amber before any ceiling is close.

Ledger and tax summary

Every expense grouped by IRS Schedule C line, with a per-year CSV export for self-filing.

Deadlines

Countdowns to the Wisconsin annual report and the federal quarterly estimates, color-coded before they are due.

Money in and out

Monthly spend by vendor against recorded revenue, plus a tax reserve and a single safe-to-draw number.

Beta broadcast

Compose, preview, and send one branded update to the beta-tester list from a real support address.

Standing AI roles

Two AI workers help run it

Beyond building features, a couple of scheduled AI assistants keep the boring, important parts from piling up. Each follows a written playbook kept in the repo.

The Accountant

Reads the LLC inbox each day, books every receipt onto the ledger already tax-categorized, and drafts a reply asking for the PDF when one is missing. Year-end filing is ready, with no scramble.

Runs dailyBooks receiptsDrafts, never sends

The Maintainer

Triages the GitHub repo every couple of days. It reads new issues, classifies them, proposes labels, drafts replies in the house voice, keeps the feature backlog, and checks deploy health.

Triages issuesKeeps the backlogProposes, never acts
Both draft and propose. Neither sends an email, closes an issue, or spends a cent on its own. A human approves everything, and every guardrail resets by hand so nothing silently resumes.
Open source

Still open source, all the way down

A company running the optional cloud does not change what ResearchOS is. The software stays free and open, and it cannot be taken away from you.

AGPLv3, forever

Free, open, and forkable. Read it, fork it, self-host it. AGPLv3 keeps it open even when it is offered as a service, so a lab-grade tool cannot quietly close up.

Your copyright, not the company's

The copyright is held by the author, Grant Nickles, not the LLC. The business cannot be used to lock the open project away from the labs that depend on it.

The company runs only the optional cloud

Every feature is free, and the local notebook never depends on the company. The only thing that can ever cost money is optional cloud storage, priced at cost.

Open, not venture-backed

ResearchOS is open source and local-first, sustained by voluntary GitHub Sponsors. There are no investors to answer to and no pressure to lock you in.
